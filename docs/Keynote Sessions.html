Keynote Speakers

Bias in Humans and AI – What To Do About It?

Professor Gianluca Demartini

University of Queensland

LinkedIn

Abstract:

The rise in popularity of general-purpose large language models (LLMs) raises questions around bias and fairness. Do these models reflect the biases and stereotypes present in the data they have been pre-trained on? What should we do about that? In this talk, reviewing recent research we conducted at The University of Queensland, we will discuss issues of bias in human data using as an example gender bias in Wikipedia, and issues of bias in AI using as an example political bias in LLMs. We will then discuss how to explore and manage such bias that exists in data and in LLMs, how these models can be used for sensitive tasks, and how users tend to trust and over-rely on AI agents, even for high-risks tasks.

Short Biography:

Gianluca Demartini is a Professor in Data Science and an ARC Future Fellow at the School of Electrical Engineering and Computer Science at the University of Queensland, Australia. He is also a Dieter Schwarz Fellow at the Technical University of Munich, Germany. His main research interests in Data Science include Information Retrieval, Semantic Web, and Responsible Artificial Intelligence. His research is currently funded by the Australian Research Council, the Swiss National Science Foundation, Meta, Google, and the Wikimedia Foundation. He received multiple Best Paper awards at Artificial Intelligence and Information Retrieval conferences. He has published more than 200 scientific papers at major computer science venues such as the ACM Web Conference, ACM SIGIR, VLDB Journal, ISWC, and ACM CHI.

Decoding the SDGs: a Bayesian Perspective

Distinguished Professor Kerrie Mengersen

Queensland University of Technology

LinkedIn

Abstract:

Bayesian methods are now pervasive in applied statistical modelling and analysis. One of the benefits - and challenges - of a Bayesian approach is understanding and formulating priors. In this presentation, I will discuss some of our applied projects in environment and health, and the characterisation of objective, semi-informative and subjective priors. Objective priors will be cast in the context of estimating the probability of presence given a sample comprising only absences and no presences. Semi-informative priors will be addressed through consideration of history matching. Subjective priors will be obtained from citizen science in the context of a Virtual Reef Diver project. The formulation of priors for estimation of various latent variable models will also be discussed.

This research is joint with a range of collaborators who will be acknowledged in the presentation.

Enabling Faithful Large Language Model Reasoning with Knowledge Graphs

Professor Shirui Pan

Griffith University

LinkedIn

Abstract:

Large language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT and Gemini have gained significant attention due to their emergent abilities and generalizability. However, as black-box models, they face limitations in capturing and accessing factual knowledge. In contrast, knowledge graphs (KGs) provide rich factual information in a structured format, enhancing LLMs' inference and interpretability. In this talk, I will present recent works on integrating KGs and LLMs for faithful reasoning. Specifically, I will introduce a KG-enhanced LLM approach, Reasoning on Graphs (ROG), which leverages knowledge graphs to enable faithful and interpretable LLM reasoning. ROG follows a planning-retrieval-reasoning paradigm: first, it enables LLMs to generate a plan to retrieve the most relevant knowledge from knowledge graphs; based on the retrieved information, LLMs can then perform faithful reasoning. To further enhance performance, I will introduce a graph foundation model that can be applied to new domains of knowledge graphs, enabling zero-shot reasoning. I will conclude with a brief discussion of future directions in this exciting field.

AI-Powered Decision Support in Health

Dr. Sankalp Khanna

CSIRO Australian e-Health Research Centre

LinkedIn

Abstract:

Artificial Intelligence is driving transformation across industries, and words like “boom”, “bubble”, and “buzz” are frequently used to describe the growing uptake of these technologies in various settings. However, health care continues to struggle with achieving sustained adoption of AI-powered solutions, despite mounting pressure on hospital systems worldwide due to ageing populations, the growing burden of chronic diseases, and skills shortages. This talk discusses the planning, development and delivery of AI-powered operational and clinical decision support solutions in the Australian health landscape by the Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation (CSIRO), Australia’s national science agency. While describing AI-powered innovation in health, the focus is on sharing the valuable lessons learnt, with researchers already working on or starting to explore this exciting area.

Short Biography:

Dr. Sankalp Khanna is a Senior Principal Research Scientist and leads the Health Intelligence team at the CSIRO Australian e-Health Research Centre. His research is focussed on applying Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning techniques to model, analyse, predict, simulate and optimise patient flow through Australian public hospitals; and develop explainable algorithms for delivering decision support that clinicians can interpret and trust. Solutions developed by Sankalp have helped reshape workflow and policy in hospitals in Australia and overseas. Sankalp has served as Adjunct Associate Professor at the Queensland University of Technology and Griffith University. He is a member and Treasurer of the Pacific Rim International Conference on Artificial Intelligence (PRICAI) Steering Committee, and a founding Fellow of the Australasian Institute of Digital Health.

Invited Speakers

Grazing The Future - Putting the AI in Agri

Dr. Kenny Sabir

AgriWebb

LinkedIn

Abstract:

Machine learning is transforming how agricultural decisions are made — not just in research labs, but on-farm. The Foragecaster program, a $7.5M, four-year Food Agility CRC collaboration led by AgriWebb with research partners including UTS, QUT, UNE Smart Farm, and Cibo Labs demonstrates how predictive models can be integrated into farm management systems to deliver value directly to producers. This talk will share the design and implementation of Foragecaster’s machine learning models across three domains: Livestock growth forecasting (leveraging over 60 million animal records from AgriWebb’s database), Pasture growth and quality forecasting (combining biophysical models, climate forecasts, and remote sensing), Sustainability analysis (including carbon sequestration, emissions efficiency, ground cover and water metrics). We will show how these predictive layers are integrated into new planning tools, such as a grazing planner, enabling producers to plan stock movements, feed budgets, and sustainability outcomes over a six-month horizon. The talk will also highlight the challenges of integrating heterogeneous data sources, the importance of explainability in agricultural ML, and lessons from working with producers through design workshops, beta farms, and deep-dive case studies. Foragecaster illustrates how large-scale agricultural datasets, machine learning, and farmer co-design can be combined to move beyond prototypes and deliver trusted, operational AI that builds resilience and sustainability in livestock systems.

Short Biography:

Dr. Kenneth Sabir, VP of R&D at AgriWebb, was instrumental as the company's first employee and former Head of Engineering, helping it grow to support 20,000 livestock producers globally. He now spearheads Foragecaster, a four-year research project with the Food Agility CRC, QUT, UTS, and industry partners, focused on developing AI-powered planning tools for improved agricultural productivity and resilience. This initiative leverages Kenneth's extensive background in delivering leading-edge software for companies like IBM and Bell Labs, where he held technical leadership roles in worldwide teams as well as researched in fields like bio-inspired computing and stream processing. His diverse prior experience also includes medical research as the Bioinformatics Architect at the Garvan Institute and has also developed software for water management with the Irrigation Futures CRC. Kenneth holds 14 patents and has authored a dozen peer-reviewed research papers across fields such as database design, data visualisation techniques, stream processing, text-mining, pasture growth ML, remote sensing, bio-inspired computing.
